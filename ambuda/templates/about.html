About

Our mailing list shares regular updates on Ambuda and our other Sanskrit projects. Our code is all on GitHub, and we eagerly welcome contributors of all backgrounds. For all other inquiries, please reach out to us directly.

Mission

Our mission is to make the Sanskrit tradition radically accessible. Specifically, we have three goals:

  1. Create a comprehensive archive of traditional Sanskrit literature. The traditional corpus is vast, but current archival efforts are piecemeal and vary widely in quality and coverage.

  2. Publish this archive in an open format. We aim to publish the entire corpus in plain text and with permissive licensing. Our user interface to this corpus will work seamlessly across devices and locales.

  3. Integrate this archive with learner-friendly tools. Most Sanskrit readers are novices, and we we can remove a lot of busywork and pointless manual toil from the learning process.

A comprehensive archive in an open format with learner-friendly tools. That's what Ambuda is for.

Roadmap

Loosely, our project has four phases. Polishing each of these phases is an unending task, but even a "good enough" version of each phase would be valuable and useful.

The phases are as follows:

  1. A delightful user interface. We will create a simple and attractive website that works across devices. Most of the work is in overall design, with a few smart features such as an integrated dictionary and on-demand transliteration to other scripts.

  2. A smart reading environment. Once the basic reading experience satisfies, we will integrate more and more assistive tooling for learners. At a minimum, we will support sandhi splitting and word-by-word analysis for at least 50% of the texts in our archive.

  3. A publishing platform. Once our project becomes the de facto way to read Sanskrit online, we will build out an end-to-end publishing platform that supports collaborative work on transcription and proofreading.

  4. A complete archive. With these three phases in place, what remains is the real work: gathering, transcribing, proofreading, and publishing all of Sanskrit.

Roughly, we expect to complete phase 1 in days, phase 2 in weeks, phase 3 in months, and phase 4 in years. Our project is currently in phase 1.

Almost all of Ambuda's data comes from other projects.

Text data mostly comes from GRETIL. For the Ramayana and Mahabharata specifically, we instead use the "official" electronic versions hosted on John D. Smith's website. As our project grows, we will include other sources of textual data and even generate texts on our own.

Dictionary data comes from the Cologne Digital Sanskrit Lexicons (CDSL) project.

Code

All of our code is available on GitHub. We use Python (Flask) for the backend, Tailwind for CSS, and native JavaScript for the rest. Our site is hosted on DigitalOcean.
